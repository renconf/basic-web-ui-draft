Winter

Winter is the coldest season of the year in polar and temperate zones. It occurs after autumn and before spring in each year. Winter is caused by the axis of the Earth in that hemisphere being oriented away from the Sun. Different cultures define different dates as the start of winter, and some use a definition based on weather.

When it is winter in the Northern Hemisphere, it is summer in the Southern Hemisphere, and vice versa. In many regions, winter is associated with snow and freezing temperatures. The moment of winter solstice is when the sun's elevation with respect to the North or South Pole is at its most negative value (that is, the sun is at its farthest below the horizon as measured from the pole), and the moment of summer solstice is when the sun is at its farthest above the horizon. The date of the winter solstice depends on the geographical location.

Winter, the coldest season in polar and temperate zones, spans about three months from the winter solstice to the vernal equinox in the Northern Hemisphere, and from the winter solstice to the autumnal equinox in the Southern Hemisphere. The season is caused by the Earth's axis tilting away from the Sun, resulting in shorter days and longer nights. The winter solstice, occurring around December 21 in the Northern Hemisphere, marks the shortest day of the year, with the sun at its lowest elevation in the sky. Weather during winter varies significantly: in temperate regions, it often brings snow and freezing temperatures, while polar regions experience extreme cold and prolonged darkness. Different cultures celebrate winter with various festivals, like Christmas and the Sapporo Snow Festival. Ecologically, many animals hibernate or migrate, and plants enter a dormant phase. Human activities during winter include skiing, ice skating, and enjoying indoor gatherings with warm foods and drinks.

Winter holidays around the world showcase a rich tapestry of cultural, religious, and seasonal traditions. December kicks off with Christmas on the 25th, celebrated globally with traditions like decorating trees, exchanging gifts, and attending church services. Hanukkah, observed by Jewish communities, begins in December with festivities that include lighting the menorah and playing dreidel games. Kwanzaa, running from December 26 to January 1, honors African heritage with principles celebrated through lighting the kinara and cultural reflection. Boxing Day on December 26, popular in the UK, Canada, and Australia, traditionally involves giving gifts to service workers and enjoying post-Christmas sales. St. Stephen’s Day, also on December 26, honors the first Christian martyr with various regional traditions. January starts with New Year’s Day, celebrated worldwide with fireworks and resolutions. Epiphany on January 6 marks the visit of the Magi to Jesus, observed with festivities like parades and special pastries in many Christian countries. Lunar New Year, which falls between January 21 and February 20, heralds the start of the lunar calendar year with family reunions and cultural festivities in East Asia. February brings Valentine’s Day, a celebration of love with gifts and cards. Carnival, held in various countries like Brazil and Italy, is a vibrant pre-Lenten festival featuring parades and masquerades. Unique regional celebrations include Japan’s Sapporo Snow Festival, famous for its snow and ice sculptures, and Scotland’s Up Helly Aa, a fire festival celebrating Viking heritage with torch-lit processions and the burning of a longship.
